# How to spot an AI: the empirical science of LLM-generated text

**AI-generated text leaves measurable fingerprints — in word choice, statistical regularity, and structural uniformity — but detecting it reliably remains a fundamentally hard problem that may become impossible.** Large-scale empirical studies analyzing millions of documents have identified hundreds of telltale vocabulary shifts, quantified the statistical flatness of machine prose, and exposed the structural rigidity LLMs impose on writing. Yet the same research reveals that paraphrasing tools, simple prompt engineering, and improving model quality can defeat every known detector. This report synthesizes peer-reviewed findings from computational linguistics, NLP, and AI detection research (2023–2026) across six domains: linguistic markers, statistical properties, structural patterns, detection methods, specific AI vocabulary, and the limits of detection.

## AI text is statistically flat and lexically predictable

The most robust empirical difference between human and AI writing is **statistical uniformity**. Humans write in bursts — mixing terse fragments with sprawling subordinate clauses — while LLMs produce text with remarkably consistent sentence length, complexity, and token predictability. Muñoz-Ortiz, Gómez-Rodríguez, and Vilares (2024), analyzing six LLMs across three model families in *Artificial Intelligence Review*, found that human texts exhibit more scattered sentence-length distributions, shorter syntactic constituents, and more optimized dependency distances. AI text maintains a predictable cadence that researchers can measure through several complementary metrics.

**Perplexity** — how "surprised" a language model is by a token sequence — is the foundational detection signal. AI-generated text has significantly lower perplexity because models select statistically likely tokens. In controlled rephrasing experiments, the autoregressive model LLaMA produced text with mean perplexity of **18.37**, while human text scored **43.03** — a gap exceeding 2×. **Burstiness**, measuring perplexity variance across a document, tells a similar story: human text scored **0.334** versus LLaMA's **0.307**, confirming that human writing fluctuates more in complexity. GPTZero pioneered combining these two metrics and remains one of few tools to formally incorporate burstiness.

**Token probability distributions** offer perhaps the clearest window into AI writing mechanics. GLTR (Gehrmann, Strobelt, and Rush, 2019, ACL) demonstrated that AI text overwhelmingly draws from the highest-probability tokens at each generation step. Their visualization — color-coding tokens by probability rank — showed AI text as predominantly "green" (top-10 predicted tokens), while human writing displays a far more diverse distribution. The practical threshold: text where **>60% of tokens fall in the top-10 predictions** strongly suggests machine origin. This occurs because LLMs are trained to maximize token likelihood, while humans routinely deploy idioms, creative metaphors, and unexpected phrasing that correspond to lower-probability choices.

**Entropy** — the uncertainty in the model's prediction at each token position — is lower and more uniform in AI text. Analysis of chain-of-thought LLM responses revealed that over **50% of tokens exhibit entropy below 0.01**, with only ~20% showing high entropy at genuine "reasoning forks." N-gram analysis reinforces these patterns: detection accuracy using n-gram frequency features climbs from 58% AUROC with unigrams to **97% AUROC with 6-grams** on standard datasets, reflecting the increasing regularity of AI text at longer sequence lengths. Both human and AI text follow Zipf's law at the token-frequency level, but with subtle exponent differences that vary by model family.

## The vocabulary of machines: 379 words that betray AI authorship

The most accessible evidence of AI writing comes from vocabulary shifts that large-scale corpus studies have now precisely quantified. Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát, and Lause (2025), published in *Science Advances*, analyzed **over 15 million PubMed abstracts** from 2010–2024 and identified **379 "excess style words"** with elevated frequencies coinciding with ChatGPT's release. This vocabulary shift was unprecedented — larger than the lexical impact of COVID-19 on scientific writing.

The excess frequency ratios for rare words are striking. **"Delves" appeared at 28× its expected frequency** in 2024 abstracts. **"Underscores" appeared at 13.8×** and **"showcasing" at 10.7×**. For common words, the excess frequency gaps were equally telling: "potential" appeared in 5.2 percentage points more abstracts than expected, "findings" in 4.1 points more, and "crucial" in 3.7 points more. The excess words were predominantly **verbs (66%) and adjectives (14%)** — unlike COVID-era excess words, which were mostly nouns — revealing that LLMs reshape how ideas are expressed more than what topics are discussed.

Juzek and Ward (2025), in the *Proceedings of COLING 2025*, corroborated and extended these findings using 26.7 million PubMed abstracts (5.2 billion tokens). Their rigorous three-step method identified **21 focal words** — including delve, intricate, nuanced, underscore, showcasing, comprehensive, multifaceted, pivotal, commendable, meticulous, realm, landscape, tapestry, crucial, and robust — confirmed through both corpus spike analysis and direct ChatGPT output testing. Critically, they investigated why this happens: the focal words were **not overrepresented in likely training corpora** (arXiv, Wikipedia), ruling out training data as the cause. RLHF-trained versions of Meta's Llama used focal words more than base models, suggesting **reinforcement learning from human feedback contributes** to these preferences, though the evidence remains mixed.

A parallel study by Liang et al. (2024) at Stanford's HAI, analyzing nearly **one million publications** from arXiv, bioRxiv, and Nature, found that **17.5% of computer science papers** and **16.9% of peer review text** contained AI-drafted content, identified partly through these same vocabulary markers. The ten highest-frequency marker words forming a reliable detection set are: **across, additionally, comprehensive, crucial, enhancing, exhibited, insights, notably, particularly,** and **within**.

Beyond single words, AI-generated text deploys characteristic phrases: "It's important to note that," "In the realm of," "Navigate the complex landscape of," "At the intersection of X and Y," "A testament to," and "In today's fast-paced world." These formulaic constructions appear with disproportionate frequency across all major LLMs. However, Li et al. (2025) documented an important coevolutionary dynamic: once "delve" was publicly identified as an AI marker in early 2024, its frequency in arXiv abstracts **began declining** — while less conspicuous AI-favored words like "significant" and "additionally" continued rising. Any specific word list has a shelf life.

## Rhetorical tics and the mechanics of AI prose style

AI writing exhibits a distinctive rhetorical fingerprint beyond vocabulary. The most thoroughly documented patterns, identified through both empirical corpus analysis and systematic close reading, reveal a prose style that is formally competent but mechanically repetitive.

**Contrastive framing** — constructions like "It's not about X, it's about Y" and "not just X, but Y" — appears in AI text roughly every 200 words, according to analysis by Pantcheva (2025) at the AI Localization Think Tank. Often the two frames lack genuine opposition, making the device vacuous. **The rule of three** operates at word, phrase, and clause levels, with AI even nesting triples within triples ("narrative, cultural adaptation, and the uneasy intersection of AI, governance, and perception"). **Fronted focus constructions** — placing a single keyword as a rhetorical question followed by elaboration ("The outcome? A burned-out team.") — and standalone "Because..." fragments used for rhetorical emphasis rather than causal explanation are additional hallmarks documented across multiple LLMs.

**Hedging language** saturates AI output. Terčon's 2025 survey, synthesizing 44 studies, confirms that AI text exhibits a more formal and impersonal style, signaled by increased nouns, determiners, and adpositions, and reduced adjectives and adverbs. Epistemic caution manifests as "It should be noted," "This suggests that," "Generally speaking," and "From a broader perspective." Muñoz-Ortiz et al. (2024) found LLMs use more numbers, symbols, and auxiliaries — suggesting objective language — while humans exhibit stronger negative emotions (fear, disgust) and less joy. AI maintains a diplomatically neutral tone that avoids strong positions.

**Punctuation tells its own story.** Em dash overuse became the most publicly discussed AI marker, but empirical testing reveals it is **model-specific, not universal**. Plagiarism Today (2025) found ChatGPT, Copilot, and DeepSeek made heavy use of em dashes, while Claude used very few, and Gemini and Meta.ai used none. Goedecke's (2025) analysis showed **GPT-4o uses roughly 10× more em dashes than GPT-3.5**, possibly due to training data drawn from late-19th/early-20th-century digitized books when em dash usage peaked. Each major LLM has distinct stylistic tells: ChatGPT favors creative, em-dash-heavy prose; Claude tends toward verbose epistemic hedging with fewer em dashes; Gemini produces more structured, list-heavy, tonally neutral content.

**Lexical diversity is consistently lower** in AI text. Terčon's survey finds AI-generated text features smaller vocabulary size and more repetitive language. Markowitz, Hancock, and Bailenson (2024), in the *Journal of Language and Social Psychology*, compared AI versus human hotel reviews and found AI text had a more analytic style, was more affective and descriptive but less readable — achieving classification accuracy exceeding **80%**. AI text also avoids first-person pronouns and personal, subjective language, instead offering abstract hypothetical examples ("A marketing team might use AI to improve their content strategy") rather than concrete specifics with names, numbers, and lived experience.

## Structural rigidity from introduction to conclusion

AI-generated documents follow organizational templates with striking consistency. Herbold et al. (2023) found ChatGPT essays exhibited **identical introductory and concluding structures** across all generated samples, with opening sentences invariably beginning with a generalized statement using the topic's key concepts. Human essays showed far greater variability in structural approach. Steere's (2024) empirical analysis of over 100 AI-generated analytical essays confirmed this: AI essays "get straight to the point," lacking the gradual build-up — personal anecdotes, definitions, rhetorical questions — common in human writing.

**Paragraph-level uniformity** is particularly diagnostic. AI constructs paragraphs with rigid consistency: a topic sentence, supporting evidence, and a summary statement. Consecutive paragraphs begin with formulaic transitions — "Firstly," "In contrast," "Furthermore," "On the other hand," "In conclusion" — creating a mechanical rhythm absent from human prose. Instruction-tuned models use participial constructions ("The system analyzes the data, revealing key insights") at **2–5 times the rate** found in human writing.

**Semantic repetition** reveals AI's inability to develop ideas. Steere documented an extreme case where a ChatGPT essay about "Life in the Iron Mills" contained, across five paragraphs, the phrases "serves as a powerful symbol," "serves as a central aspect," "serves as a constant reminder," "serves as a catalyst," "serves as a microcosm," and again "serves as a powerful symbol" — essentially restating the same claim six times with minor variation. Shaib et al. (2024, Northeastern University) quantified this systematically: **~76% of syntactic templates** in model-generated text matched patterns in pre-training data, versus only **35% in human-authored texts**. The gap was largest in creative genres (movie reviews, news) and smallest in biomedical writing, where human authors already follow rigid style guides.

AI text also exhibits **perpetual balance** — presenting multiple perspectives even when unnecessary, creating prose that feels "wishy-washy." It favors breadth over depth, producing competent surface-level coverage without genuine critical analysis. Arguments sound familiar but break no new ground. When personal reflection is requested, AI remains in third person, producing what Steere calls "dispassionate prose." It "overexplains" with unnecessary appositives defining well-known figures and deploys hyperbolic descriptors — "groundbreaking," "vital," "invaluable," "profound" — for mundane topics.

## The detection toolkit: from watermarks to probability curvature

Detection research has produced an evolving arsenal of methods, each with distinct strengths and fundamental trade-offs. The most promising approaches fall into three categories: zero-shot statistical methods, watermarking, and supervised classification.

**Zero-shot methods** require no training data and exploit the statistical properties of LLM outputs directly. DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023, ICML) established the foundational insight that LLM-generated text occupies **negative curvature regions** of the model's log-probability function — random perturbations of AI text tend to have lower log probability than the original, while perturbations of human text show no such pattern. This improved GPT-NeoX-20B fake news detection from **0.81 to 0.95 AUROC**. Fast-DetectGPT (Bao et al., 2024, ICLR) replaced the expensive perturbation step with conditional probability curvature, achieving **~75% relative improvement** over DetectGPT while running **340× faster**. It correctly flags 80% of ChatGPT content while misidentifying only 1% of human text.

The current zero-shot state of the art is **Binoculars** (Hans et al., 2024, ICML), which computes the ratio of log-perplexity from an "observer" LLM to cross-perplexity between two related models. This elegantly solves what researchers call "the capybara problem" — perplexity depends on topic, so unusual subject matter produces high perplexity regardless of authorship. Binoculars normalizes for context, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy and proving "particularly strong at low false positive rates" on the RAID benchmark.

**Watermarking** offers the most reliable detection when available. Kirchenbauer et al. (2023, ICML) proposed partitioning the vocabulary into "green" and "red" token sets using pseudo-random functions seeded by preceding context, then boosting green-token logits during generation. Detection uses a statistical z-test: watermarked text shows significantly more green tokens than the ~50% expected by chance. The method is detectable with as few as **~50 tokens** and has "negligible impact on text quality." However, it requires provider cooperation, is vulnerable to paraphrasing, and adversaries can potentially infer and spoof watermark signatures.

**Supervised classifiers** — including Turnitin's AIW-2 system, RoBERTa-based detectors, and RADAR — learn complex feature combinations from labeled data. Turnitin has processed over **250 million submissions** and claims <1% false positive rates, though independent evaluations dispute this. The RAID benchmark (Dugan et al., 2024, ACL) — the most rigorous cross-detector evaluation, using **6.2+ million generations** across 11 models, 8 domains, and 11 adversarial attacks — revealed sobering results: detectors trained on ChatGPT were "mostly useless" against text from other LLMs, and simple adversarial attacks (extra spaces, homoglyph substitution, synonym replacement) rendered most detectors ineffective. At operationally useful false positive rates (<5%), most tools' claimed accuracies collapsed.

## Why reliable detection may be impossible

The most consequential finding in this literature is that **perfect AI text detection faces fundamental theoretical limits**. Sadasivan et al. (2023, published at ICLR 2024) proved that the AUROC of the best possible detector is bounded by the Total Variation distance between human and AI text distributions. As LLMs better emulate human writing — shrinking this distance — even optimal detectors deteriorate. Achieving a 90% true positive rate with a 1% false positive rate becomes **mathematically impossible when the distributions overlap more than 11%**.

Practical attacks already exploit this theoretical vulnerability. Krishna et al. (2023, NeurIPS) built DIPPER, an 11-billion-parameter paraphrasing model that drops **DetectGPT accuracy from 70.3% to 4.6%** at 1% false positive rate without meaningfully degrading text quality. Lu et al. (2023) demonstrated SICO, a prompt optimization method requiring only 40 human-written examples and ~$1 in API costs that decreases detector AUC by **0.5 on average** across six detectors — and once constructed, works universally. Even simpler approaches succeed: asking ChatGPT to "write like a teenager" reduced Turnitin's detection rate from **100% to 0%** in Times Higher Education testing.

**False positives represent the most documented harm.** Liang et al. (2023, *Patterns*) evaluated seven widely-used detectors on TOEFL essays written by non-native English speakers and found an average false positive rate of **61.3%** — over half of genuine human essays misclassified as AI-generated. **97.8% of TOEFL essays** were flagged by at least one detector. The root cause: non-native writers' constrained vocabulary and simplified grammar produce low-perplexity text that mimics AI statistical signatures. AI detectors have also famously labeled the U.S. Constitution as AI-generated — because its heavy representation in training data gives it artificially low perplexity.

Weber-Wulff et al. (2023, *International Journal for Educational Integrity*) tested 14 detection tools on 54 documents and concluded they are "neither accurate nor reliable," with all tools scoring below 80% accuracy. While tools identified human text with 96% average accuracy, they caught ChatGPT text at only 74% — falling to **42% after slight editing**. Mixed human-AI text, where authors use AI for some portions, remains an essentially unsolved problem. Most detectors assume text is entirely human or entirely AI, an assumption that "fails to reflect real-world usage patterns."

## Conclusion

The empirical picture is clear but paradoxical. AI-generated text carries numerous measurable signatures — **statistical flatness** (lower perplexity and burstiness), **vocabulary compression** (379 identified excess words, with "delves" at 28× expected frequency), **structural rigidity** (formulaic paragraphs, semantic repetition, balanced-to-a-fault argumentation), and **rhetorical predictability** (contrastive framing, hedging saturation, rule-of-three patterns). These markers are real, well-quantified, and empirically robust across studies analyzing millions of documents.

Yet detection remains fundamentally unreliable for high-stakes decisions. The theoretical ceiling proven by Sadasivan et al. means that as models improve, the detection problem gets harder by mathematical necessity. Practical evasion is already trivial — paraphrasing, prompt engineering, or light editing defeats most tools. False positive rates devastate vulnerable populations, particularly non-native English speakers. The most honest assessment comes from the RAID benchmark: at operationally acceptable false positive rates, no existing detector performs reliably across models, domains, and adversarial conditions. The coevolutionary dynamic — where identified markers get suppressed while subtler ones persist — ensures this remains a moving target. Detection tools provide useful probabilistic signals but should never serve as sole evidence of AI authorship.
